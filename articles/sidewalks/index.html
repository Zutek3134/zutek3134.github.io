Problems With Sidewalks In Taiwan　

2022/06/24 ~ 9 min 社會

There are many images in this article, and it might impact the speed of loading the page.

Have you ever experienced walking on the road, with cars roaming next to you, as if you're a superhero with flapping capes?

Have you ever seen sidewalks that just end suddenly and forces you walk on the road?

Is it true that the roads are filled with cars, and the sidewalks are filled with motorcycles?

Well, that is the typical scene in Taiwan. Every time I encounter such problems, I cannot help but sigh that it is such a dangerous thing to just merely walk. To walk safely is so difficult, you might as well fly -- flying might even be safer.

I. Traffic　

The most important part of traffic is "pedestrian area," which is just a fancy way to call "sidewalk."

Sidewalk is the base for other traffics related to pedestrians. Therefore, how can you build other facilities if you do not even have a sidewalk?

If there is a street without sidewalks, or there are, but just blocked by various obstacles, pedestrians would have no choice but to walk on the road, with all the cars.

It's not that there are no sidewalks in Taiwan, it's just that they are so rare, and the existing ones are terrible. In fact, most drivers lack the knowledge to "leave sidewalks to the pedestrians."

With so many factors intertwined, Taiwan's title of "Pedestrian Hell" is well deserved.

II. What's Wrong With Sidewalks In Taiwan　

Sidewalk problems in Taiwan can be categorised as follow: with the worst being "no sidewalks at all," the most common "sidewalks, but it's actually a pedestrian arcade," and the one with the highest rarity "complete sidewalk."

"Sidewalks, but it's actually a pedestrian arcade" is mostly the case here. The arcade actually belongs to the shop or stand, and that is why they put a bunch of personal stuffs there, blocking the entire path.

Walking there is just like parkour, get distracted and you've walked into a dead end. Hiding and dodging, sometimes going on the road.

With spaces full of obstacles just like life, people with limited mobility are even more limited.

"Pedestrian arcades" however, counts as a private land in terms of property rights, why must they offer the public to walk? We do not have to share our private property with the public, right?

“Since the pedestrian arcade is a road, its owner has the obligation to provide public access from the start. In principle, no stalls could be set up without permission.”

—— The Republic of China Constitutional Court Reporter Interpretations No. 564. August 8th, year 92 of the Republic of China (2003)

According to the constitutional interpretation, although the arcade is technically a private land, it is still required to make room for the public to walk.

Despite those, the government are still required to pave the way for people on wheelchairs and babies in carts.

Problems with arcades, appear in normal sidewalks, too. Think of it as the same parkour game, but different maps.

Besides the vehicle that park as they wish, there are also gigantic trees just literally in the middle of the road, poles, lamps, electricity boxes, broken tiles, some furniture of nearby households... etc.

III. What The Government Did　

Now, the issues with sidewalks are so obvious, the government cannot possibly miss it, right? If that's the case, shouldn't they do something?

Yeah, and they did. Yet, as you can probably tell, they did not do a good job. It seems that they are not good at fixing the bugs in their land. They are, however, excellent at making new (and worse) problems!

Oh, that's not what they thought. In their terrible bug fix update release log, they claimed to have implemented sidewalks so long that you can walk around Taiwan 5.3 times. Here's a poster they made:

Since 2010, Taipei has been drawing "marked sidewalks." Just paint a green path, then write "sidewalk (人行道)," and finally add a drawing of an adult holding a children. Simple as that.

People seem to like the novel design of a drawn sidewalk. However, it did not take long before someone figured out a new way to "use" the sidewalks.

Anyways, more and more cities started painting sidewalks on the streets... uh but they did not plan the locations of the sidewalks carefully.

Among all the marked sidewalks, some are cut down in the middle, some went through obstacles, some even took up 90% of the roads.

IV. How They Affect People　

Before online class, I had to walk to school every morning, and walk back home every evening. To me, sidewalk played an indispensable role in my daily routine.

Since 2016 and until now (2022), I have changed my path to school 6 times so as to commute safer. Mathematically, I find a new way annually.

Except the reson which I discarded the first and original route for being way too long, the rest were all because of "too inconvenient."

The original path was to walk on crowded streets, which is where sidewalks would exist. But I had to go across many blocks, it's far and time-consuming.

The new sidewalks I walk later on, were either terribly broken, heavily waterlogged, full of scooters, or not-existing.

Depart 6 in the morning, return 6 in the evening, both being the peak hours of streets usage. Why should students participate in ultimate obstacle courses every day?

V. Ever-lasting Problems of Sidewalks　

How are such obvious and important issues never resolved?

Is it because no one is capable of doing so?

If "to walk safely" is an unanimous demand of our people, how can it be possible for this problem to continue existing?

The only reasonable eplanation, is that "someone" does not want it to be fixed.

In fact, the local government is doing the opposite, from allowing motorcycles to be parked on arcades to setting up parking spaces on sidewalks.

What about the people? Well, they acquiesced.

Basically, most people commute by car or bus. These things run on the road, and the wider the it is the better.

Taiwan motorcyclists are known for zig-zagging around the streets. There is no gap they cannot slip through.

If the motorcyclists can do it, so must pedestrians. After all, it is much better to ride on roads rather than on sidewalks, so of course they dislike sidewalks.

The government blamed sidewalks for causing traffic jams and took it down; the shops, in order to make money, thinks that sidewalks makes it difficult for customers to park and agrees the removal.

In this era when the minority obeys the majority, their voices are often ruthlessly swallowed.

VI. Conclusion　

Commercial interests and environmental policies are often at odds with each other.

Just like the "Kyoto Protocol," which was designated to combat global warming. After the leaders created a goal, they quickly found that the domestic economy was seriously affected, and the US, a signatory, even withdrawn.

Despite all, I personally see something in the marked sidewalks, and believe that with proper planning and positioning, it can be very helpful.

The current problem is that it cannot withstand wind, rain, and time.
[Img 16] Something probable

Human-oriented transportation has changed from previously "vehicle"-based concept to a "human"-based thinking mode.

The city's route space and transportation should cooperate with each other so as to create a suitable road environment, and neither passers-by nor drivers will have to suffer.

Although it is impossible to implement immediately, the government can still try and renovate existing sidewalks, and plan carefully before making new sidewalks in the future.
