Is It Football or Soccer⚓︎

2022/11/20 ~ 7 min 語言 社會

The World Cup has kicked off and every team has its fans. But there's one word that keeps people arguing - is it football or soccer? Which side are you on?

Well it seems like most people that speak British English say "football," and American English users use "soccer."

This never-ending debate may not be as exciting as the World Cup, but it must be more interesting than you've imagined.

I. What's The Difference⚓︎

Sport
football soccer
rugby football

Since there are two words representing the same thing, and both claiming to be the "correct" one, let's explore the etymology.

II. How It Began⚓︎

Firstly we have "soccer" from Team US!

Let's start with a quick history lesson.

Our dear soccer was born in... England?

Yes, England! The British invented the word "soccer"!

Wait, invent?

It turns out that this word was not technically "naturally evolved," and it is more appropriate to call it a "popular term."

During the second half of the 19th century, it is common in England, especially Oxford, to extract a syllable from a word and append "er" to the end. "Five dollars" becomes "fivers," for example.

In the meantime, people had to distinguish between Association Football and Rugby Football, and therefore "association" was simplified into "assoc" (which sounds like "a sock"), then "soccer." Some called rugby as "ruggers" too.

Frankly, no one ever used the full term "Association Football." The upper class tends to use the trendy "soccer," while the middle class sticked with "football."

As to the United States, they've established the "American Football Association" in 1913, and later renamed it to the "United States Soccer Football Association" in 1945. Finally, after 29 years, they dropped the word "football" and remained the "United States Soccer Federation" until today.

Did you know...

"FIFA" is short for "Federation Internationale de Football Association."

The same applies to Canada. Just swap out "United States" and replace it with "Canadian."

With this, all kinds of football at that time could be easily told apart. Many regions started to adopt this new name.

Soccer brought the English-speaking world together.

III. How It Unfolds⚓︎

In the middle of the 20th century, the upper class declined.

Their power quickly went downhill, losing the ability to influence the society. As they were vanishing, they have brought the term "soccer" away with them as well. The middle class, along with "football," won the favour of the British.

People in England went back to using "football," yet no one notified the Americans!

The Americans were still using the "popular word 'soccer,'" completely unaware that many in England were promoting the "correct term."

The British labelled soccer as "incorrect" and "American" - while that's not actually the case.

Did you know...

During the same period, Australia and New Zealand were also active in "resorting back to football", trying to replace "soccer" with "football." However, things in Australia was not as smooth as New Zealand. (How were they notified anyway?)

It seems like soccer has torn the English-speaking world apart... or united the British English-speaking world against the American English-speaking world.

IV. How It's Used⚓︎

1. English-speaking Countries⚓︎

In countries where English is the official language, the name for football can vary depending on the region.

Places like the UK, India, Hong Kong, and Singapore, mostly in Europe, Asia, Africa, usually call it "football." North America and Australia says "soccer."

There's Australian football in Australia, and they just refer to it as "football." Even though the Prime Minister says soccer is wrong and that it's football, Australians still call it soccer. The football team is even nicknamed as "Socceroos."

In Ireland, both football and soccer are used.

2. Other Countries⚓︎

(1) Transliteration⚓︎

Many languages just directly transliterate "football" (some "soccer") to represent football.

Japanese has many ways of saying football, the most common one is "サッカーsakka." Japan Football Association, however, uses the word "Football" in its English name and "サッカーsakka" in the Japanese name. Other ways they call football are "フットボールfuttobooru," "アッソシエーションassoshieeshyon", "蹴踘kemari", "ア式蹴球ashikishiuukyuu."

In Cantonese, "踢波ti buo" is used. The Chinese character 踢ti means kick and 波buo means wave, 波buo is used just because it sounds similar to "ball."

(2) Paraphrase⚓︎

Some languages literally translated the word "football," like in Chinese "足球zu qiu," or Dutch "voetbal."

(3) Metonymy⚓︎

There are also languages that directly use existing vocabularies to mean football.

In Korean 축구chuggu (or 蹴球chuggu) means to "kick ball."

"Calcio" in Italian came from the verb "to kick," which is "calciare."

"Nogomet" in Bosnian, Croatian, and Slovenian is a combination of "noga (leg)" and "met (throw)," which means "to throw the ball with the legs."

Swiss-German "schútte" or "tschuutte" both come from the English "shoot".

V. Conclusion⚓︎

This article has explored the various ways in which different languages represent the sport of football. While "football" and "soccer" refer to the same sport, there are notable differences in how they are used and understood around the world.

At the end of the day, whether you prefer to kick it as football or score a goal as soccer is absolutely fine. Both came from the same origin, just widely used in different regions now.

To me, the term "football" makes more sense since it accurately describes the sport where players primarily use their feet to kick the ball. However, I also recognize that language is constantly evolving and it is important to respect regional differences in terminology.
